Predictability arising from randomness? What is this, did I somehow reverse-engineer Desmos's RNG, or is this actually real?

While reverse-engineering RNG would have been really cool, no, I didn't have to do that for this graph—all I needed was statistics. Just like the physical variant shown to the right, this digital Galton board (also called a quincunx or bean-machine) demonstrates two statistical phenomenon: regression to the mean and central limit theorem.

First, I'll explain regression to the mean—the balls are less likely to bounce towards the outside since there are literally less paths that take the balls there. Consider a 4-tiered pegboard like the default configuration for this graph. To reach the leftmost slot, the ONLY way to get there is to bounce left four times, which has a 1 in 16 probability if bouncing left is equally as likely as bouncing right. By contrast, there are 6 unique ways to reach the middle slot (can you count them all?)—a 6 in 16 probability. As a result, the balls are biased to be closer to the center.

Secondly, central limit theorem—it is well known among staticians that taking many random samples from an unknown distribution will commonly approximate a normal distribution. Our Galton board with 100 balls is really just 100 random samples from an unknown sampling, so central limit theorem applies in this case. As a result, both my digital version with perfect binomial distribution AND the physical board with chaotic physics-based distribution produce similar bell curves, even though the individual balls don't actually have the same behavior.
